# Proposal Voice Guide

This skill is a knowledge layer. Commands and skills that produce client-facing documents read this before writing.

## Voice Principles

**Confident, not pushy.**
State what you do and what it means for the client. No begging, no hedging, no "we hope to" or "we believe we can." Instead: "Here is what we deliver" and "Here is what changes for you."

**Specific, not vague.**
Numbers, timelines, and deliverables beat adjectives. Replace "significant improvement" with "30% reduction in processing time." Replace "extensive experience" with "147 implementations across 12 industries."

**Their language, not yours.**
Mirror the exact words the client used on the discovery call. If they said "customer churn," do not write "client attrition." If they said "pipeline," do not write "sales funnel." Check call notes before writing.

**Short sentences.**
If a sentence has a comma, consider splitting it. If a paragraph has more than 4 sentences, it is too long for a proposal. Bullet points over paragraphs. Three bullets max per slide.

## Specificity Standards

| Vague (reject) | Specific (accept) |
|----------------|-------------------|
| "Significant ROI" | "3.2x return within 6 months" |
| "Industry-leading" | "Ranked #1 by G2 in Q4 2025" |
| "Extensive experience" | "147 implementations since 2019" |
| "Improved performance" | "Page load time from 4.2s to 1.1s" |
| "Reduced costs" | "$340K annual savings on infrastructure" |
| "Several clients" | "23 enterprise clients including [names]" |

## Structure Non-Negotiables

1. **One point per slide or section.** If it has two, split it.
2. **Lead with what matters to the buyer,** not what matters to you. Their problem before your solution.
3. **Headlines are conclusions, not labels.** "Reduce churn by 30%" not "Our Approach." "Ship 2x faster with half the bugs" not "Development Methodology."
4. **Every deck ends with specific next steps and dates.** "Kickoff call: March 15. First deliverable: April 2." Never "Let's discuss next steps."
5. **The first slide after the cover addresses their specific problem.** Not your company history.

## Copy Non-Negotiables

- No jargon the client has not used first
- No superlatives (best, leading, world-class, cutting-edge, innovative, revolutionary)
- No passive voice in CTAs: "Schedule the kickoff" not "The kickoff can be scheduled"
- No weasel words: "up to," "as much as," "potentially," "may help"
- Numbers always beat words: "3 weeks" not "a few weeks"
- Consistent tense throughout (present or future, never mixed)
- Client name spelled correctly everywhere (search for every occurrence)
- Dates in the client's local format
- Currency with proper symbols and formatting

## The One-Point Rule

Before finishing any slide, section, or email: ask "What is the ONE thing the reader should take away?" If the answer is not immediately clear, rewrite. If there are two takeaways, split into two sections.

## Case Study Format (when included)

1. **Challenge:** One sentence. What the client was struggling with.
2. **Approach:** Two sentences max. What you did differently.
3. **Result:** Specific numbers. Revenue, time saved, cost reduced, conversion improved.
4. **Quote:** (optional) One sentence from the client in their words.

Total: 4-6 sentences. Never more than half a page.

## Conclusion Headlines

The final section headline should be a forward-looking statement, not a generic label:

| Reject | Accept |
|--------|--------|
| "Conclusion" | "Your path to 40% faster releases" |
| "Summary" | "What changes for [Client] in 90 days" |
| "Next Steps" | "Kickoff March 15, first results by April" |

## Pre-Send Checklist

Before any document goes to a client:
- [ ] Client name spelled correctly in every occurrence
- [ ] All numbers are accurate and sourced
- [ ] No superlatives or vague claims
- [ ] Headlines are conclusions, not labels
- [ ] Ends with specific next steps and dates
- [ ] Consistent tense throughout
- [ ] Reviewed for jargon the client has not used

## Do NOT
- Send a proposal without re-reading it from the client's perspective
- Use your company's internal terminology in client-facing documents
- Include a slide or section that does not directly serve the client's decision-making
- Copy-paste from a previous proposal without updating every client-specific detail
- End with "Questions?" as the final slide. End with next steps.
