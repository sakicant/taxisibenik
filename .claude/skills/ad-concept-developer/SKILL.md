# Ad Concept Developer

Creative mechanics are the structural patterns that define how an ad creates meaning. They are not hooks (what you say) and not formats (what it looks like). They are the underlying mechanism: the cognitive or emotional move the ad makes that causes the viewer to feel, conclude, or remember something.

## Where Mechanics Sit

```
Messaging Angle (from creative strategy)
    ↓
Creative Mechanic (this skill)
    ↓
Hook (opening line that triggers the mechanic)
    ↓
Visual Format (production structure)
```

## Before Developing

Confirm you have:
1. **Messaging angle** — the core truth for this audience intersection
2. **Awareness stage** — where the audience is in their journey
3. **Persona** — who you are talking to
4. **Product** — what you are advertising

If a creative strategy matrix exists, reference it.

## The Mechanic Library

### The Implied Answer
The hook poses a question. The visuals silently answer it. No one states the answer out loud. The viewer concludes it themselves.

**Why it works:** Self-conclusions bypass ad skepticism. When viewers connect the dots, the insight lands as their own thought.

**Best for:** Unaware, Problem-Aware

**Structure:** Hook = question that sounds like an observation. Visual = the silent answer. Brand never states the connection.

**Example:** "Why do you always stay home?" / Visual of someone in an incredible home office setup / The product made their space so good they prefer it.

### The Social Witness
Someone other than the customer notices the change. A compliment, a double-take, an unsolicited "what are you doing differently?" The product's impact is validated through a third party's reaction.

**Why it works:** Third-party validation is more believable than self-reported results. It implies the change is visible enough that others notice.

**Best for:** Problem-Aware, Product-Aware

**Structure:** Customer living normally → someone else notices and comments → customer's response is casual → product appears naturally.

### The Overheard Conversation
Framed as something you were not supposed to see. A text thread, a DM, a group chat. It feels like eavesdropping, not advertising.

**Why it works:** Removes the "this is an ad" filter. Viewers process it as social content. Informal framing makes claims more credible.

**Best for:** Unaware, Problem-Aware

**Structure:** Frame as text/DM/chat → one person mentions a problem → the other casually recommends → ends like a real conversation.

### The Reframe
Opens by validating a belief the viewer holds, then flips the frame entirely. The new perspective is the hero, not the product.

**Why it works:** People share things that changed how they think. The Reframe manufactures that "I never thought about it that way" moment.

**Best for:** Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware

**Structure:** Validate conventional wisdom → show they did everything right → reveal the real problem is something else → product solves the real problem.

### The Borrowed Enemy
Describes a problem obviously caused by a specific competitor without ever naming them. The viewer makes the connection themselves.

**Why it works:** Naming a competitor triggers defensiveness. Not naming them but making the reference unmistakable lets the viewer do the conquesting.

**Best for:** Solution-Aware, Product-Aware

**Structure:** Describe the problem in vivid, specific detail → use language that only applies to the competitor → position your product as the natural alternative.

### The Trojan Horse
Looks like educational content or a personal story until the final 20%, where the product appears naturally as the resolution. The viewer is engaged before they realize it is an ad.

**Why it works:** Ad avoidance is highest at the moment of recognition. Delaying that recognition until the viewer is invested means the product reveal feels earned.

**Best for:** Unaware (cold audiences)

**Structure:** Open as pure content → build genuine value → product enters naturally as part of the resolution → no hard sell.

### The Contrast Without Comment
Shows two realities side by side (before/after, with/without) but never tells the viewer which is better. The visual makes it obvious.

**Why it works:** Stating the obvious feels like an ad. Showing it and letting the viewer conclude feels like evidence.

**Best for:** Problem-Aware, Solution-Aware

**Structure:** Split screen or sequential cut → problem state shown neutrally → solution state shown neutrally → no voiceover judgment.

## Developing a Concept

1. Start with the messaging angle
2. Ask: how do I want the viewer to arrive at this truth? That is your mechanic.
3. Select the mechanic that best serves the awareness stage and persona
4. Write the hook that opens the mechanic
5. Describe the visual/narrative structure beat by beat
6. Note what format best delivers it (video, static, carousel, etc.)

## Layering Mechanics

The strongest concepts often combine a primary mechanic with a secondary one:
- Implied Answer + Social Witness
- Trojan Horse + Reframe
- Overheard Conversation + Social Witness
- Contrast Without Comment + Borrowed Enemy

One mechanic shapes the concept architecture. The other adds depth.

## Output Format

For each concept:
1. **Mechanic:** which one (and secondary if layered)
2. **Messaging angle:** the core truth
3. **Awareness stage:** target
4. **Hook:** opening line with trigger label
5. **Beat sheet:** 3-5 beats describing what happens visually and narratively
6. **Format recommendation:** video / static / carousel / etc.
7. **Why it works:** one sentence on the cognitive move
